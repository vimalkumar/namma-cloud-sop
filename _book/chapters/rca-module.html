9  Root Cause Analysis (RCA) Module

9.1 Purpose

The RCA Module defines how the Namma Cloud team investigates incidents, identifies root causes, implements corrective actions, and prevents recurrence. Every incident — no matter how small — is an opportunity to improve.

Our RCA Philosophy

We don’t do RCA to assign blame. We do RCA to understand systems, improve processes, and grow as a team.

9.2 When to Trigger an RCA

An RCA must be initiated for:

RCA Trigger Events
Trigger Example Timeline
Hardware damage Server damaged during transport RCA within 24 hours
Installation failure Server doesn’t fit in rack RCA within 48 hours
Configuration error Wrong network port connected RCA within 48 hours
Safety incident Near-miss or actual injury RCA within 12 hours
Repeat issue Same problem occurs twice RCA within 24 hours
Process deviation SOP not followed RCA within 48 hours
Unexpected downtime Server fails after installation RCA within 24 hours

An RCA should be considered for:

  • Maker-Checker failures (task rejected by Checker)
  • Near-misses that could have become incidents
  • Process inefficiencies discovered during work
  • New learnings that reveal gaps in SOPs

9.3 The RCA Process

9.4 Step-by-Step RCA Guide

9.4.1 Step 1: Immediate Response (Within 1 Hour)

When an incident occurs:

  1. Ensure safety — If anyone is at risk, address safety first
  2. Stop the activity — Don’t continue with the task
  3. Preserve evidence — Take photographs, don’t clean up or fix anything yet
  4. Document immediately:
    • What happened?
    • When did it happen?
    • Where did it happen?
    • Who was involved?
    • What was being done at the time?
  5. Notify the Team Lead
  6. File an incident ticket
Quick Documentation

Use your phone to:

  1. Take photos of the incident
  2. Record a voice note describing what happened (in any language)
  3. Note the exact time

This can be formalized later, but capture it NOW while it’s fresh.

9.4.2 Step 2: Gather Evidence (Within 24 Hours)

Collect all relevant information:

  • Photographs (before, during, after)
  • Checklists (were they completed? any items missed?)
  • SOP version that was being followed
  • Statements from team members involved
  • Environmental conditions (temperature, humidity, lighting)
  • Equipment specifications
  • Any prior similar incidents
  • Maker-Checker sign-off forms (if applicable)

9.4.3 Step 3: Build a Timeline

Create a chronological timeline of events:

Focus on: What happened just before the incident? What changed?

9.4.4 Step 4: The 5 Whys

The 5 Whys technique helps us dig deeper than the surface-level cause:

5 Whys Guidelines
  • Keep asking “Why?” until you reach a systemic cause (process, training, tool gap)
  • If you find yourself blaming a person, ask “Why?” again — there’s always a system behind the human action
  • Sometimes you need more than 5 Whys, sometimes fewer. The number is a guide, not a rule.
  • Multiple root causes are possible — investigate all branches

9.4.7 Step 7: Update SOPs & Checklists

If the RCA reveals an SOP gap:

  1. Draft the SOP update
  2. Review with Team Lead
  3. Get approval
  4. Publish the updated SOP
  5. Brief the team on the change
  6. Record the change with version number

9.4.8 Step 8: Share Learnings

Every RCA culminates in a learning session:

  1. Present the incident (what happened)
  2. Walk through the 5 Whys
  3. Explain the root cause
  4. Describe the CAPA
  5. Show the updated SOP/checklist
  6. Open for questions and discussion
  7. Key message: “This is how we get better, together”

9.7 Real-World Example: Server Damaged During Transport

Case Study: RCA-001

Incident: During transportation from the guest house to the data center, one HP server was found to have slight physical damage upon arrival.

5 Whys:

  1. Why was the server damaged? → It shifted during transport
  2. Why did it shift? → Packaging was not snug around the server
  3. Why wasn’t packaging snug? → Generic padding was used instead of custom-fit packaging
  4. Why was generic padding used? → No packaging standard existed for this type of transport
  5. Why was there no standard? → The transportation SOP did not include packaging specifications

Root Cause: Transportation SOP lacked specific packaging requirements for different server types and transport scenarios.

CAPA:

  • ✅ Created detailed packaging specifications (see Transportation SOP)
  • ✅ Added shock and moisture indicators to packaging checklist
  • ✅ Added Maker-Checker process for packaging verification
  • ✅ Created separate packaging guidelines for branded vs assembled servers
  • ✅ Conducted training session on proper server packaging

Result: No further transport damage incidents after implementing these changes.

Remember

“Every incident we investigate and learn from makes our next operation better. RCA is not punishment — it’s our most powerful learning tool.”
